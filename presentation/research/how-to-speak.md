## 1. SLIDE DESIGN

### Words per slide / "don't read your slides"
- **Core axiom:** we have one language processor — read OR listen, not both. Too
  many words forces reading and the speaker is tuned out.
- Slides EXPOSE ideas; the speaker CONVEYS them. The slide is a prop.
- Cut almost all words. Optional: lean live deck + word-rich handout.

### Font size minimums
- Minimum **40–50 pt**. Rationale: large fonts mechanically force word removal.
- "35 pt begins to get too small." (OCW PDF.)

### Clutter / logos / title bars
- **Remove the title from every content slide** ("I'm telling you the title").
- **Remove logos** — "background junk including logos is always a distraction."
- Remove decorative backgrounds, footer bars, bullet ornaments.
- Heuristic: thumbnail all slides — if the deck looks "heavy," cut more.

### Graphics vs text
- Prefer simple, purposeful images over clipart/decoration.
- Striking graphics can carry an idea words cannot.

### Laser pointers
- **Never use a laser pointer** — you lose audience contact, waving an unstable dot.
- Instead: bake a **static arrow/highlight into the slide** at the point of interest.

### Number of slides
- Rule of thumb **~1 slide / minute** (ceiling, not target). For 5 min → ~5.
  Treat as a ceiling; a defense committee tolerates a few more if each is one idea.

### Hapax legomenon — the one complex slide
- **At most ONE genuinely dense/complex slide** per talk. Everything else: simple,
  single-concept. For a 5-min defense → zero, or one if method/arch truly needs it.

### "Heckler-proofing"
- The **Contributions** final slide stays on screen during Q&A and re-asserts the claim.
- Each slide should stand alone visually so a zoned-out committee member re-engages.

## 2. TALK STRUCTURE

### Opening: the "empowerment promise" (NOT a joke)
- **Never open with a joke** — audience not attuned; it lands flat / signals insecurity.
- **Open with a promise:** what the audience will know/be able to do by the end.
- A surprising statistic/fact is also an acceptable opener (curiosity hook).

### "The cycle" — repeat core idea 3–5×
- ~20% of the audience is mentally absent at any moment — cycle on the main idea
  from different angles so it lands for everyone.

### "Build a fence" — distinguish from prior work
- State explicitly what your idea IS NOT, and its similarities/differences with
  nearest prior work, so the committee cannot conflate your contribution.

### Verbal punctuation / landmarks
- Every ~5 min give a landmark: enumerate what's covered, signal what's next.
- Enumeration ("first… second… third…") IS verbal punctuation.

### Asking a question
- Pose a moderately difficult question; **wait 7 seconds**. Re-engagement hook.

### Time and place
- Ideal 11 AM, well-lit, full-ish room. Defense slot is fixed, but: arrive early,
  case the room, test the projector, adjust lighting.

---

## 3. CLOSING

### Final slide = Contributions
- NOT "Thank you", "Questions?", "The End", blank, URL, or collaborator list.
- Lists what you did / showed / why it matters. Stays up during ALL Q&A.
- Collaborators/acknowledgments go at the START if anywhere.

### Final words — the "salute", not "Thank you"
- **Never close with "Thank you"** — implies people stayed out of politeness.
- Alternatives: (1) a brief joke (people remember the last minutes); (2) a sincere
  **salute** — value of the place/work/opportunity. For a conservative committee,
  the salute is safer; reserve humor only if natural and brief.

## 4. SHORT TALK / JOB TALK → THESIS DEFENSE MAPPING

5-minute job-talk structure (maps 1:1 to a defense):
1. **Vision** = problem + novel approach (first 60–90 s).
2. **Accomplishment** = what you did / results (enumerate steps).
3. **Contributions** = list them explicitly (final slide, stays up for Q&A).

The defense is a **persuasion** context, not information transfer. Practice with
uninformed reviewers who will criticize — surfaces gaps in the "fence."

### Winston Star (make the idea stick): Symbol, Slogan, Surprise, Salient idea, Story
- Symbol: a signature diagram. Slogan: one punchy sentence (use in the promise).
- Surprise: lead with the unexpected number/result.

## DIRECT APPLICATION TO OUR 5-MIN BEAMER DECK

### DO
- Open by stating the empowerment promise verbally — "In 5 minutes I'll show you
  [specific claim]." No joke.
- ~5–7 slides, each = one idea. Diagrams ARE the content (aligns with "more diagrams").
- Body text 40+ pt minimum. Strip Beamer theme of logo/footline/headline bars.
- 1–3 word labels on content slides, not sentences. Speak the context.
- No laser pointer — bake arrows/circles into slides for callouts.
- At most ONE dense diagram (architecture or key result). All else simple.
- Final slide = Contributions (past tense, 3–5 items). Leave up for Q&A.
- Close with a salute, not "thank you." Then stop.
- Verbal punctuation at ~halfway: "We've seen the problem and approach — now results."
- Build the fence early (slide 2–3): one sentence vs nearest prior work (DefGuard).

### DO NOT
- No opening joke / "thank you for the opportunity" preamble.
- No >~20 words body text per slide; bullets are not the primary content mode.
- No "Questions?"/"Thank You" final slide. No closing "thank you" words.
- No laser pointer. No per-slide logos. No >1 dense slide. Don't read slides aloud.
